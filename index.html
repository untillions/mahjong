Terms to Know

Let's begin with the game itself: Mahjong 麻将 (má jiāng: sounds like "maw-zhong").

Pronunciation

On this page you will see Chinese characters, "sounds like" english pronunciations, and Pinyin 拼音 (sounds like "pin-yin").

Pinyin (literally meaning "spelled sounds") uses the roman alphabet to spell out Chinese sounds. There are 4 different accents which represent the tone to be taken by the syllable. See below.

The Suits

There are three suits (Characters, Bamboos, and Circles). Reading a tile in any of these suits is as simple as saying the number + the suit name together. Examples are shown below.

Characters Suit

萬 (wàn: sounds like "Juan")

In the characters suit, the character 萬 (wàn, meaning ten thousand) appears on the tile below each number.

Example: qī wàn ("chee Juan")

Bamboos Suit

索 (tiáo: sounds like "tsea-ow")

In the bamboos suit, the yī tile usually depicts a bird rather than a single bamboo stick and the bā tile usually is formed like a diamond, rectangle, or set of 'M's. The tiles can be distinguished by the number of bamboo sticks.

Example: èr tiáo ("er tsea-ow")

Circles Suit

筒子 (tǒng: sounds like "tong")

In the circles suit, all tiles can be distinguished by the number of circles.

Example: wǔ tǒng ("wuh-oo tong")

Setting up the Hand

Game Organization

A traditional game of Mahjong is 4 rounds with 4 hands in each round. Rounds follow the order of the winds (as does gameplay) in the following sequence:

East → South → West → North

Order of Play

To begin, choose a side of the table to be "East". Players draw one of the 4 wind tiles and arrange themselves around the table based on the counterclockwise order of winds.

Note: This arrangement is different than the arrangement of cardinal directions on a compass.

This drawing of winds and reseating around the table happens at the beginning of each round.

Note: Some variations of the game do not redraw winds at the beginning of each round. The person who initially drew East, remains East in all 4 rounds.

Making the Wall

All 144 tiles are placed face down and washed (shuffled). Each player takes out 36 tiles and stacks them face down in a row of tiles 18 long and 2 high. The rows are then arranged in the pattern below to form the wall.

Breaking the Wall

For the first hand of the first round, the player in the East position becomes the dealer. This player rolls three dice and finds the sum. Starting with their own east wall as “1”, the player counts upward in a counterclockwise direction around the circle until they reach the section of wall that corresponds to the sum of the dice.

Example: The East player rolls a sum of 6 which results in the South wall being broken.

The player on that section of the wall counts tiles from right to left on their wall until reaching the sum of the dice rolled. That player then breaks the wall to the left of this point (making a visible separation).

Example: The South player (from their point of view) breaks the wall after the 6th tile from the right counting left.

Note: Some variations of the game require the person who will break the wall to reroll a sum rather than using the East player’s sum of dice.

Dealing the Tiles

The player who broke the wall takes the next two stacks (four tiles) to the left of the break and adds them to their hand. Moving around the table counterclockwise, each player continues taking the next two stacks (four tiles) until all players have 16 tiles in their hand.

Note: Some variations of the game deal the tiles differently or end up with a different sum of tiles initially.

Once tiles have been drawn, the wall has been opened. The remaining tiles form a shape that is representative of 门 mén (meaning "gate").

The wall now has a head (the starting point of the first stroke to draw the character) where players will continue drawing tiles as the game goes on, and a tail (the ending point of the final stroke to draw the character) where players will draw replacements for a flower tile, season tile, or gàng.

Replacements

Players arrange their hands with the tiles stood up facing them. Players sort the tiles by suit in order 1-9 and put the honors together.

If any flower or season tiles have been drawn, these are played face up on the table in front of the player’s rack, and players take one replacement tile at a time from the tail of the wall (in turn counterclockwise) until all players have 16 non-flower, non-season tiles in their hand. If a player draws a flower or season tile from the tail, this player will still have a replacement to draw (in turn with the other players). When drawing single tiles from the wall, always take the top tile from the end stack first, then the bottom tile.

Example: The East, West, and North players are showing flowers and seasons. These players will draw replacement tiles one-at-a-time from the tail to match the number of flowers or seasons removed from their hand in this order: East player, West Player, North Player, West Player, North Player.

Setting Up Later Hands

After each hand, if the dealer declares hú, the starting position does not move. This player rolls the dice again for their second, third, or more times as dealer.

For any hand that the dealer did not hú, the starting position in the next hand passes to the next player counterclockwise. This player then rolls the dice, indicates which wall will be broken, and the player on that side of the wall will break again and begin the dealing of the tiles. Once all players have been dealer in a round, it is time to move to the next round.

At the beginning of each consecutive round, the East player becomes dealer again.

Gameplay

The Goal

In a game of 16 tiles, be the first to create a hand of 5 sets and/or sequences of 3 (called melds) and 1 pair. This requires 17 tiles total to win.

Note: Another variation uses 13 dealt tiles and one less meld.

Melds

In a hand of 16 tiles, players are aiming to create 5 melds (sets or sequences) and 1 pair.

Sets

Sets are three of the same tile.

Sequences

Sequences are three tiles in numerical order (suited tiles only).

Pairs

Pairs are two of the same tile. A pair could become a set if a third identical tile is drawn or claimed, or it can remain a pair to fulfill the winning hand condition.

Drawing and Discarding

A typical turn consists of two steps:

  1. Draw a tile from the wall (or claim a discard).
  2. Discard a tile (also called throwing).

Always draw new tiles from the head of the wall. Always discard tiles face-up into the center of the table.

Note: Proper game etiquette could include announcing the discarded tile by its name and arranging discarded tiles neatly so all players can easily track what has been thrown.

Claiming Discards

There are three ways to claim tiles that other players discard, bypassing the standard wall draw.

Chī 吃

If a player discards a tile that could complete a sequence in the next player’s hand (counterclockwise), that player can claim it instead of drawing from the wall.

To do so, the next player must simply announce “Chī”, reveal the two matching sequence tiles from their hand, and place all three tiles face-up in front of them. This player then discards a tile as usual, and play continues to the person on their right.

Note: Players can only chī from the player sitting directly before them in the standard turn rotation.

Pèng 碰

If any player discards a tile that could complete a set of 3 in another player’s hand, that player can claim it—even if it is not currently their turn in the rotation.

To do so, the player must announce “Pèng”, reveal the two identical tiles from their hand, and place all three tiles face-up in front of them. This player then discards a tile as usual, and play continues to the right of this player (regardless of how many players may have been "skipped" between the original player who discarded and the player who declared pèng).

Gàng 槓

If any player discards a tile that could complete a set of 4 in another player’s hand, that player can claim it immediately, regardless of turn order.

To do so, the player must announce “Gàng”, reveal the three identical tiles from their hand, and place all four tiles face-up in front of them. This is known as an open gàng. Because an extra tile is locked up in a set of four, the player must draw a replacement tile from the tail of the wall before discarding a tile as usual. Play then continues to the person on their right.

Special Gàng Conditions

The standard claim results in an open gàng. However, a closed gàng can also be declared directly from a player's hidden hand.

If a player draws the fourth tile of a set already in their hand, they have two options:

  1. Declare it as a closed gàng: The player places all four tiles face-down in front of them (sometimes flipping the two outer tiles face-up to show the set identity). They draw a replacement tile from the tail of the wall, discard a tile, and play continues to their right. Declaring a closed gàng safely increases the hand's score if they achieve hú.
  2. Use it in a sequence: Keep the tile hidden and use it as part of a separate consecutive sequence (suited tiles only). This requires no declaration, keeps a player's strategy secret, and helps the player move closer to a generic win.

Additionally, if a player already has an exposed pèng face-up on the table and they draw the fourth matching tile themselves, they can add it to the meld to upgrade it to an open gàng. They must then draw a replacement tile from the tail and discard.

Note: Players are never allowed to use a tile discarded by another player to upgrade an existing face-up pèng.

Hú 和

When a player is exactly one tile away from winning (a state known as tenpai), they gain the ability to claim a discarded tile from any player to complete their hand—whether it finishes a sequence, a set, or their final pair. This is the only scenario where a pair can be completed using a discarded tile.

Multiple Claims

If two or more players attempt to claim the exact same discarded tile simultaneously, priority is given based on the following hierarchy:

PriorityActionCondition Allowed
1HúCompleting a winning hand
2Gàng / PèngCompleting sets of 3 or 4
3ChīCompleting a sequence of 3

Note: If multiple players can declare hú off of the same discarded tile, multiple players win the hand.

Winning the Hand

A player completely wins the hand by securing 5 valid melds and 1 pair. If the final piece is claimed from another player’s discard pile, the player calls out “hú” to claim it and win. If the final piece is successfully drawn from the wall by the player themselves, they declare “zìmō”, which yields a higher point payout during the scoring phase.

Special Situations

Yellow Hand

If no player is able to complete a winning hand by the time all wall tiles are drawn and discards have been made, the hand is declared a yellow hand. No one wins and the dealer position rotates.

Note: Some variations create a carry over pool (such as 1 base per person) payable to the next self-draw winner.

Scoring

Payouts

In Taiwanese Mahjong, scoring is calculated in tai 台 points.

Depending on a player’s hand and the conditions under which a player wins, the player can earn more tai and therefore a higher payout.

Note: Many groups play with a 5-tai minimum to win, but some allow 0-tai wins (simply completing a winning hand).

Once a winner is declared, find the sum of tai earned by the winner of the hand. If the winning tile came from a discarded tile of another player, that player who discarded the winning tile pays the hand winner in the amount of tai earned. If the winning tile was self-drawn from the wall (zì mō), all players each pay the hand winner in the amount of tai earned.

Base

Depending on house rules, some tables may use an initial base for any winning hand. For example, a hú by a non-dealer on a no-streak dealer hand with no other special conditions would earn a base of 3 tai.

Dealer Bonuses

When a dealer wins a hand, they earn tai based on their winning streak. For the first hand they win, they receive 1 tai. In every subsequent win, the dealer bonus increases by 2 tai. Therefore, the number of tai a dealer earns based on the number of hands they have won in a row, n, can be found with:

2n – 1

End of Game

Keep track of tai payouts in every hand (including subtracting the points from the payer and adding the points to the payee). Once the game is finished, the total tai for each player (positive or negative) determines the winner and/or how much actual money (if gambling) should be paid out.
